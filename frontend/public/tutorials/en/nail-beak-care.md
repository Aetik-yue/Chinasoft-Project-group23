# Nail Trimming and Beak Care

Overgrown nails can interfere with perching or catch on fabric and cause a fracture. Beak abnormalities can make eating difficult. This guide explains how to tell when trimming is needed, how to trim safely, and how to care for the beak day to day.

## How to Tell When the Nails Need Trimming

- The tips of the nails curl noticeably when the bird stands on a flat surface.
- The toes twist or the bird cannot stand steadily while walking.
- Its grip scratches you painfully.
- The nails catch on clothing or towels.
- Check the nails about once every 1–2 months.

> Nail growth and natural wear vary from bird to bird. Birds whose nails receive less natural wear may need more frequent trimming.

## Trimming Tools

- Curved nail clippers made for birds; do not use human nail clippers.
- Styptic powder ready at hand (cornstarch can be used in an emergency).
- A towel for gentle restraint.
- Good lighting, ideally with two people working together.

## How to Trim

1. Gently wrap the parrot in a towel, leaving only one foot exposed.
2. Examine each nail carefully and avoid the pink blood supply, called the “quick.”
3. Trim only the pale portion beyond the quick, leaving a 1–2 mm margin.
4. The quick is difficult to see in dark nails, so remove only a tiny amount at a time.
5. Check after every cut. If you are unsure, trim less.
6. Check for bleeding afterward. If bleeding occurs, apply firm pressure with styptic powder.

## Routine Beak Care

- A beak wears down naturally; a healthy bird does not need routine beak trimming.
- Provide a mineral block or cuttlebone for natural wear.
- Chew toys also help keep the beak at an appropriate length.
- A thin surface layer of keratin may flake off; this is normal.

## Beak Abnormalities That Need Attention

- An overgrown or crossed beak (“scissor beak”): may indicate a nutritional or congenital problem.
- Peeling or softening of the beak surface: may indicate vitamin A or calcium deficiency.
- A chipped or broken beak: may result from injury or a nutritional problem.
- Thickening or crusting on the beak: may indicate mites.
- A sudden color change: may be a sign of liver dysfunction.

Seek veterinary care for any of the above. Do not try to reshape the beak yourself with scissors.

## Why You Should Not Trim the Beak Yourself

- The beak contains blood vessels and nerves. An incorrect cut can cause severe bleeding and intense pain.
- Beak trimming is a medical procedure and should be performed by a veterinarian.
- Improper trimming can also cause permanent deformity.

## Preventing Overgrown Nails

- Provide natural-branch perches in different diameters to encourage normal wear.
- Place a nail-conditioning perch where the bird steps on it regularly, but do not make the bird stand on it all the time, as this can injure the feet.
- Do not place a nail-conditioning perch beneath the food bowl, where it would cause prolonged pressure on the feet.
- Make regular nail checks part of your routine.

## Reassurance After Trimming

- Offer a treat afterward to build a positive association.
- Return the bird to its cage to rest; do not force immediate interaction.
- If bleeding does not stop, seek veterinary care immediately.
- The toes may grip less securely for a short time after trimming and should recover within half a day.

> Tip: For the first nail trim, ask a veterinarian or experienced bird handler to demonstrate. Learn to identify the quick before trimming on your own, and always remove too little rather than cutting into the quick.
