# How to Tell Whether Your Parrot Is Healthy

Parrots instinctively hide discomfort as a survival strategy in the wild. By the time a bird looks “obviously unwell,” its condition may already have worsened. Spending two minutes on the following observations each day can help you detect and address problems early.

## Check Alertness and Behavior

A healthy parrot usually:

- Has bright eyes and responds to sounds and movement.
- Enjoys climbing, chewing toys, and preening.
- Stands upright and grips firmly with both feet.

Watch carefully for the following warning signs:

- Remaining on the bottom of the cage.
- Fluffed feathers and the head tucked beneath a wing.
- No response to normally favored foods or sounds.
- Obvious tail bobbing, which is a sign of labored breathing.

## Check the Droppings

Normal droppings have three components:

1. **Dark fecal portion:** Digested food.
2. **White urates:** Normally white or cream-colored.
3. **A small amount of clear urine:** This should not be excessively watery.

Warning signs include:

- Watery droppings or green or black feces.
- Urates that turn yellow, red, or green.
- Droppings stuck around the vent, creating a “dirty bottom.”
- No droppings for several consecutive hours.

> Note: The first dropping in the morning is often larger and darker, which is normal. Assess the overall pattern throughout the day.

## Check Feathers, Skin, and Breathing

- Feathers should be neat and glossy, without obvious breaks or bald patches.
- Feather loss during a molt is normal, but a large bald patch can indicate feather plucking.
- The nostrils should be clean, open, and free of discharge.
- The beak surface should be smooth, without abnormal thickening or peeling.
- The beak should remain closed during breathing, with no wheezing audible.
- Persistent open-mouth breathing is dangerous and may indicate oxygen deprivation or fever.

## Check Eating and Drinking

- Is food intake stable? Record any sudden decrease.
- Has water intake increased sharply? This may indicate diabetes or kidney disease.
- Does the bird select only one food? Long-term dietary imbalance can cause malnutrition.

## Keep Weight Records

Weigh the bird daily or every other day, ideally at a fixed time before the morning meal:

- Use a digital kitchen scale accurate to 1 g.
- A loss of more than 10% over a short period usually signals a health problem.
- A continuing record reveals trends; a single measurement has limited meaning.
- You can maintain these records in the app under “Pet Profile → Weight Records.”

## When Immediate Veterinary Care Is Essential

- Persistent open-mouth breathing or pronounced tail bobbing.
- Inability to stand steadily or falling from a perch.
- Complete refusal of food for more than 12 hours.
- Heavy bleeding or trauma.
- Neurological signs such as seizures, head tilt, or circling.

> Tip: Do not begin medication on your own after noticing an abnormality. Diagnosis by an avian or exotic-pet veterinarian is the most reliable approach, so keep the contact information for your nearest clinic ready in advance.
