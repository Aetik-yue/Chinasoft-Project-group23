# Going Out and Using a Travel Carrier

Taking a parrot outside carries real risks, including escape, fright, temperature changes, and exposure to pathogens. With the right equipment and methods, however, an outing can also be enriching. This guide explains how to travel safely with your parrot.

## Why a Travel Carrier Is Essential

- Carrying a parrot outdoors on your shoulder creates an extremely high escape risk; one sudden fright can send it flying away.
- Traffic, birds of prey, cats, and dogs all create hazards outdoors, so the parrot needs physical protection.
- A travel carrier gives the bird a “safe room” and reduces stress.
- In some areas, regulations also require pets to be properly contained while away from home.

## Choosing a Travel Carrier

- It should be large enough for the bird to turn around and extend its wings, but not so large that it can crash about inside.
- Choose sturdy metal or hard plastic that cannot be chewed through.
- The door must latch securely, ideally with a second lock.
- The bottom should have a tray to contain droppings.
- It should include removable food and water dishes.
- Avoid transparent soft-sided carriers, which may leave a parrot feeling exposed and insecure.

## Preparing Before the Outing

- Acclimate the parrot to the carrier in advance. Leave it accessible at home for several days and place treats inside so the bird can enter and leave on its own.
- Confirm that the door is locked and the food and water are secured.
- Bring a small bag of extra food and some wet wipes.
- Check the day’s weather and avoid extreme temperatures and strong wind.
- Avoid crowded or noisy times and locations.

## Precautions While Outside

- Hold the carrier or wear it securely at all times; never set it casually on the ground, where there may be dogs, cats, or vehicles.
- Do not let strangers pet the bird freely, as they may transmit pathogens.
- Do not remain beside a road for long; exhaust fumes and noise are harmful.
- Account for temperature changes and give the bird time to adjust when moving from an air-conditioned room outdoors.
- Provide shade in strong sunlight to prevent heatstroke.

## Traveling by Vehicle

- **Bus or subway:** Confirm in advance whether pets are allowed and prepare the carrier before boarding.
- **Private car:** Secure the carrier on the rear seat, not on the front passenger seat where an air bag creates a risk.
- **Long-distance travel:** Offer water every 2–3 hours and avoid prolonged sun exposure.
- **Air travel:** Arrange the required health or quarantine certificate in advance and confirm the airline’s rules.

## Visiting a Veterinary Clinic

- Use a travel carrier instead of holding the bird in your hands.
- Line the carrier with cloth; other sick birds may be present at the clinic, so prevent direct contact.
- Wash and disinfect the carrier after returning home.
- In a waiting room, stay as far from other birds as practical.

## Outdoor Free Flight?

- Outdoor free flight is strongly discouraged, even if the bird’s wings have been trimmed.
- One gust of wind or a bird of prey can frighten it into flying far away.
- Wing trimming is not insurance; escape remains possible.
- If you want the bird to experience flight, use a secure indoor space.

## Responding to Fright

If the parrot becomes frightened while outside:

1. Cover the travel carrier immediately with a cloth to create darkness and a sense of security.
2. Move to a quiet location and reassure the bird softly.
3. Do not open the carrier door right away.
4. Let the bird rest fully after returning home and monitor its alertness and behavior.

> Tip: Make the first outing short and quiet—for example, ten minutes in a residential garden—and increase the duration and distance gradually.
