# Summer Cooling and Winter Warmth

Parrots are very sensitive to temperature changes. More than the absolute temperature, **large temperature swings** and **direct drafts** are likely to make them ill. For most small parrots, such as budgerigars, cockatiels, and green-cheeked conures, the most comfortable range is 20–26°C. This guide explains seasonal environmental management.

## Suitable Environment

- Temperature: 20–26°C
- Humidity: 40–60%
- Keep the temperature change within one day under 8°C whenever possible.

Maintain good airflow, but never allow air to blow directly onto the cage. A thermometer and hygrometer near the cage are more reliable than judging by feel.

## Preventing Summer Heat Stress

- Keep the cage out of direct sunlight; use a shade curtain or move it to a cool, well-ventilated area.
- Provide a shallow bathing dish so the bird can cool itself. The water should be no deeper than its ankles.
- A bowl of ice water near the cage can help cool the surrounding air, but never blow icy air directly at the bird.
- **Do not** aim an air conditioner or fan directly at the cage.
- Food spoils quickly in summer. Discard moist food, such as fruit and vegetables, if it has not been eaten within two hours.

### Signs of Heatstroke

If the parrot is breathing with its beak open, holding its wings away from its body, or becoming lethargic, it may be overheated:

1. Move it immediately to a cool, shaded, well-ventilated place.
2. Lightly mist the feet and the undersides of the wings to help it cool down.
3. Do not pour ice water over the bird; a sudden temperature change can cause shock.
4. Even if it appears to recover, a veterinary examination is still recommended.

## Keeping Warm in Winter

- Secure a ceramic heat emitter or heating pad on the **outside** of the cage to prevent burns.
- A thick cage cover can be used at night, but leave an opening for ventilation.
- **Do not** place the cage directly in front of a heating vent, which can make the air excessively dry.
- **Do not** wrap the cage in an electric blanket; this can cause overheating or inadequate ventilation.
- Maintain indoor humidity. Use a humidifier if needed, but never direct its mist at the cage.

### Choosing Heating Equipment

| Equipment | Best use | Precautions |
|---|---|---|
| Ceramic heat emitter | Stable nighttime warmth | Must have a protective guard to prevent burns |
| Heating pad | Localized warmth at the bottom | Secure outside the cage and leave an unheated area |
| Cage cover | Nighttime insulation | Leave an opening for ventilation |
| Room air conditioner | Whole-room temperature control | Avoid direct airflow |

## Seasonal Transitions

Large temperature swings in spring and autumn make respiratory illness more common:

- Observe behavior and droppings more frequently during these periods.
- Reduce open-window time if necessary and prevent cross-drafts.
- Molting requires extra energy, so provide appropriate nutritional support.
- Watch the difference between daytime and nighttime temperatures and cover the cage earlier in the evening when needed.

## Damp-Season Protection

- Humidity that remains above 70% encourages mold and respiratory problems.
- Use a dehumidifier or the dehumidifying mode on an air conditioner.
- Change the cage liner frequently to prevent mold.
- Store food in sealed containers to prevent dampness and spoilage.

> Tip: A thermometer and hygrometer with data logging lets you review the day’s highest and lowest readings and makes hidden risks easier to spot.
