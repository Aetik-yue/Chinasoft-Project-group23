# Molting Care Essentials

Parrots generally molt once or twice a year, usually in spring and autumn. Molting consumes substantial energy, so a bird may become more irritable, sleep more, and scatter feathers everywhere. This guide explains how to help your parrot through the molt comfortably.

## Normal Signs of Molting

- Many feathers fall out, with down feathers and flight feathers visible on the cage floor.
- “Pin feathers” appear on the crown and neck. These look like small white tubes and contain new feathers as they grow.
- The bird preens more often and may occasionally remove an old feather.
- Appetite may increase slightly because feather growth requires energy.
- The bird may become more irritable or more attached than usual; either can be normal.

## Nutrients to Emphasize

The need for protein, calcium, and fat increases during a molt:

- Increase protein moderately with cooked egg yolk once or twice a week, unsalted tofu, or a small amount of boiled chicken breast.
- Provide calcium by hanging a cuttlebone or bird-safe mineral block in the cage for the bird to chew.
- Provide vitamin A and essential fatty acids with small amounts of carrot, pumpkin, and flaxseed.
- Continue the staple diet; pellets are more balanced than seeds alone.

> Note: Calcium should be provided in moderation because excessive amounts can burden the kidneys. Letting the bird regulate its own intake from a cuttlebone is the safest approach.

## Bathing and Humidity

- Feathers become drier and itchier during a molt, so increase misting to 2–3 times per week.
- Room-temperature water is sufficient; do not use very hot water.
- Make sure the bird dries completely after bathing to prevent chilling.
- Hold the mister at least 30 cm away; the finer the mist, the better.

## Reducing Stress

- Energy is lower during a molt, so avoid wing trimming, changing cages, and long-distance travel.
- Provide 10–12 hours of quiet, dark sleep at night.
- Do not change food frequently; digestive stability supports the molting process.
- Reduce forced interaction and give the bird space to retreat when it chooses.

## Watch for Abnormal Feather Loss

The following are not part of a normal molt and need investigation:

- Large bald areas with exposed skin.
- Plucking that causes bleeding.
- A stalled molt in which new feathers fail to grow.
- Lethargy, refusal of food, or weight loss at the same time.

Possible causes include malnutrition, parasites, feather-plucking disorder, environmental stress, and endocrine disease.

## Caring for Pin Feathers

- New pin feathers are fragile; do not pull or pinch them hard.
- Remove sharp objects from the cage so that new feathers are not broken by rubbing against them.
- Keep the number of perches moderate to reduce collisions.
- If a keratin sheath does not release from the skin, soften it with a fine mist rather than pulling it away.

> Tip: Gentle morning sunlight during a molt, while avoiding midday sun, supports vitamin D production and feather health.
