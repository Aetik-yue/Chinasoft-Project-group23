# Training Basics: Step-Up and Returning to the Cage

Training is not about making a parrot “perform.” It builds trust and makes everyday care, such as nail trimming and giving medicine, easier. This guide covers two essentials: stepping up and returning to the cage.

## Preparing to Train

- Training works best when the parrot is alert and slightly hungry, such as before a meal.
- Prepare favorite treats—hemp seeds, sunflower seeds, or dried apple—cut into small pieces.
- Choose a quiet environment, close all doors and windows, and remove distractions.
- Train for 5–10 minutes at a time, once or twice a day, without exhausting the bird.
- Patience matters most. If the bird does not succeed, make the task easier rather than punishing it.

## Step 1: Let the Bird Become Comfortable with Your Hand

- Hold your hand still near the cage so that the bird can observe it and become accustomed to it.
- Repeat this every day until the bird no longer avoids your hand.
- Next, place a treat in your palm and hold it just inside the cage door, waiting for the bird to approach voluntarily.
- Do not follow the bird around with the food; allow it to choose to come closer.

## Step 2: Teach “Step Up”

- Hold a treat in one hand. Place the other hand flat in front of the bird with the palm upward, using a finger as a perch.
- Lure the bird onto your hand: let it see the treat in your other hand, positioned so that it must step onto your finger to reach it.
- The moment it steps up, give the treat and praise it.
- Do not force it to remain if it steps down; simply repeat the exercise.
- Once the behavior is reliable, add a cue such as “step up.”

## Step 3: Train the Bird to Return to Its Cage

A return-to-cage cue is important for everyday management:

1. Use the same cue, such as “home,” every time the bird enters its cage.
2. Reward it with a treat immediately after it goes inside.
3. At first, use a treat to guide the bird to walk back through the cage door by itself.
4. Gradually reduce the lure until it responds to the verbal cue alone.
5. Never associate returning to the cage with punishment, or the bird will resist going back.

## Advanced Skill: Target Training

- Use a small wooden stick and reward the bird whenever it touches the tip with its beak.
- You can later use the target to guide it to move, turn in a circle, or pass through a hoop.
- Targeting is the foundation for many complex behaviors and provides substantial mental exercise.

## Common Problems

- **It bites and will not let go:** Do not jerk your hand away, which reinforces the lesson that biting makes the hand disappear. Stay calm and redirect its attention with a treat.
- **It calls constantly during training:** It may be overexcited or too hungry; adjust the timing and intensity.
- **Progress has stalled:** Return to the previous step, reduce the difficulty, and reinforce that stage for several days before advancing.
- **It responds to only one person:** Ask other household members to participate in feeding and training so the bird builds trust with several people.

## What to Avoid During Training

- Never flick the beak, hit, or scold the bird; this destroys trust.
- Do not force training while the bird is ill or tired from molting.
- Do not continue for too long; training while exhausted causes regression.
- Do not use food deprivation, which damages health over time.

> Tip: End every session on a successful moment, not after the bird has become frustrated. This helps it look forward to the next training session.
