# Parrot Emotions and Body Language

Parrots cannot speak our language, but they express their emotions through their bodies. Learning to read these signals helps you tell whether your bird is happy, nervous, or unwell—and prevents you from realizing it was upset only after being bitten.

## Signs of Happiness

- Feathers are slightly fluffed yet relaxed, and the body posture is at ease.
- Gently grinding the beak, as if chewing, indicates contentment and security.
- The tail moves slightly and the eyes look soft.
- The bird approaches you voluntarily and eats food from your hand.
- It occasionally makes soft cooing sounds or sings.
- It lowers its head for a scratch—one of the clearest signs of trust.

## Signs of Nervousness or Fear

- The body becomes rigid, feathers lie close to the body, and the neck stretches upward.
- The pupils suddenly contract and dilate, indicating excitement or alertness.
- The wings press tightly against the body and the tail moves rapidly.
- The bird backs away and tries to increase the distance from you or from something nearby.
- It screams sharply or hisses.
- It makes “sham-eating” movements, repeatedly working its beak when nervous.

When you see these signals, stop approaching and give the bird space.

## Signs of Anger or Warning

- The feathers stand on end, especially over the crown and neck.
- The beak opens and makes a clicking or snapping sound.
- The pupils contract to pinpoints.
- The body leans forward and the wings open slightly.
- The bird stamps its feet and bobs its body up and down, preparing to attack.

Do not reach toward the bird at this point. Wait for it to calm down; forcing contact is very likely to result in a bite.

## Signs That It Wants Attention

- It taps the cage or a toy gently with its beak.
- It calls toward you, then becomes quiet as soon as you approach.
- It climbs to the cage door on its own.
- It pushes a toy to the side of the cage.
- It lowers its head to ask for a scratch.

These behaviors are its way of saying, “Come spend time with me.”

## Signs of Illness or Discomfort

- The feathers remain fluffed for a long time and the bird appears listless.
- It keeps its eyes closed and tucks its head beneath a wing.
- It stands motionless on the bottom of the cage.
- The tail bobs noticeably with each breath, which can indicate difficulty breathing.
- It loses weight and the keel bone becomes prominent.
- It refuses food or its appetite drops sharply.

If any of these signs appears, consult *How to Tell Whether Your Parrot Is Healthy* and consider seeking veterinary care.

## Courtship Behavior

- A male may display his feathers, dance, or mount a toy or your hand.
- A female may look for a corner in which to nest and may shred paper.
- Some birds regurgitate food for you as a courtship gesture.
- Territorial behavior may increase, and the bird may attack other people.
- Providing a longer period of dark, uninterrupted sleep can help reduce excessive hormonal behavior.

## What to Do If You Are Bitten

- Do not shout or flick the bird; that can reinforce the association “bite = reaction.”
- Calmly return it to the cage and end the interaction.
- Consider whether you missed any warning signals immediately before the bite.
- Record the circumstances leading up to the bite so that you can identify its triggers.

## Key Points for Interacting with People

- Respect the bird’s “no,” and do not force petting when it is tense.
- Observe its posture before approaching and leave it enough space to move away.
- Reward relaxed behavior and voluntary approaches to build a positive cycle.
- Every bird has a different personality; do not compare yours with someone else’s.

> Tip: A parrot’s mood can change quickly. It may be happy one moment and startled by a sound the next. Keep observing, and do not push contact after it has already given warning signals.
