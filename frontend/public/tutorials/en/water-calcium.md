# Water and Calcium Essentials

Water and calcium are two of the most frequently overlooked elements of parrot care. Water must remain clean and fresh, while calcium should be provided consistently but in appropriate amounts. This guide explains how to offer both.

## Basic Drinking-Water Requirements

- Replace the water with fresh water every day, ideally once in the morning and once in the evening.
- Use cooled boiled water or purified water rather than untreated tap water, which may contain chlorine and metals.
- Wash the water dish every day to prevent bacterial biofilm.
- Water spoils more quickly in summer, so replace it 2–3 times a day if needed.
- Keep the water dish in the shade; direct sunlight encourages algae growth.

## Choosing a Water Dish

- Ceramic or stainless steel resists chewing.
- Choose a stable, weighted design that the bird cannot tip over.
- The opening should be moderate in size: a large opening becomes dirty easily, while a very small one is difficult to drink from.
- A ball-valve water bottle is another hygienic option, but the bird must be trained to use it.
- Keep food and water containers separate so that food does not fall into the water.

## How to Estimate Water Intake

- Observe changes in the water level while accounting for evaporation.
- The amount of clear urine in the droppings also reflects water intake.
- A sudden sharp increase in drinking may indicate kidney disease or diabetes.
- If the bird stops drinking completely, investigate immediately because illness may be present.

## Why Calcium Matters

- Calcium is essential for bones, feathers, the beak, and eggshells.
- Deficiency can cause weak bones, soft-shelled eggs, and seizures.
- Parrots cannot produce calcium themselves and must obtain it from food.
- Requirements increase during growth, molting, and breeding.

## Ways to Provide Calcium

- **Cuttlebone:** The safest method. Hang one in the cage so the bird can chew it and regulate its intake.
- **Mineral block:** Provides calcium and other minerals, but do not confuse it with a salt-containing “salt block.”
- **Eggshell powder:** This can be made at home, but the shells must be washed, baked dry, and ground finely.
- **Calcium powder:** Mix it into soft food according to the product directions; do not exceed the recommended amount.

## Misconceptions About Calcium

- More is not always better. Excess calcium can cause kidney stones and calcification of soft tissues.
- Calcium alone is not enough; vitamin D3 is also needed for absorption.
- D3 can be produced naturally through safe sunlight exposure or supplied with a bird-specific D3 supplement.
- An unbalanced calcium-to-phosphorus ratio, especially high phosphorus and low calcium, undermines supplementation, so limit phosphorus-rich seeds.

## When Additional Calcium May Be Needed

- During molting, because feather growth uses calcium.
- In egg-laying females, because eggshells require large amounts; deficiency can cause soft-shelled eggs or egg binding.
- During growth in young birds, for skeletal development.
- During recovery from illness, when directed by a veterinarian.

## Do Not Use These Calcium Sources

- Human calcium tablets, which may contain unsuitable additives and dosages.
- Milk, because parrots cannot digest lactose and may develop diarrhea.
- Salted bones, which contain too much sodium.

## Daily Water and Calcium Checklist

- Replace the water and wash the water dish every day.
- Keep a cuttlebone available in the cage.
- Offer calcium-rich dark vegetables, such as broccoli and kale, once or twice a week.
- Add extra calcium powder during egg laying when appropriate.
- Track weight and arrange regular health checks to monitor overall nutrition.

> Tip: Cuttlebone is inexpensive and safe, making it the first choice for calcium. If the bird will not chew it, scrape some into powder and mix it with a favorite soft food.
