# Your Parrot’s First 7 Days at Home

The first seven days after bringing a parrot home are the most important adjustment period. Its surroundings, sounds, smells, and feeding routine have all changed, so stress is common. This plan will help the transition go smoothly and prevent problems caused by excessive interaction or sudden diet changes.

## Prepare Before the Parrot Arrives

Set up the environment before bringing the parrot home:

- Place the cage in a quiet area with soft light and good ventilation but no direct draft, away from the kitchen and balcony door.
- Install the food and water dishes, perches, and toys in advance, and check that nothing has a sharp edge.
- Obtain the same staple food used by the previous owner. Any diet transition should be gradual to avoid digestive upset.
- If you have other pets, such as cats or dogs, plan a separate area before the parrot arrives.

## Day 1: Minimize Disturbance and Observe

- Once the cage is in place, avoid moving it again.
- After providing food and water, avoid reaching into the cage unnecessarily.
- At night, cover three sides with a cage cover to create a sense of security.
- Confirm that the bird can find the food and water dishes, and record how much it eats and drinks on the first day.

## Days 2–3: Confirm Eating, Drinking, and Droppings

- Check morning and evening whether the seed or pellets have decreased.
- Droppings should be formed and include white urates. Contact an avian or exotic-pet clinic promptly if diarrhea or persistently watery droppings appear.
- Is the bird alert? Are its eyes bright? Is it huddled in a corner for long periods?
- Do not move the cage or force the bird to come out during these days.

## Days 4–5: Begin Gentle Interaction

- Sit beside the cage and speak softly so the bird becomes familiar with your voice and presence.
- Place a small treat, such as hemp seed or dried apple, in your palm at the cage door and wait for the bird to approach voluntarily.
- Move slowly. Do not reach down from above its head, which can be frightening.
- If it is not ready to take food from your hand, do not force it. Continue calmly for several more days.

## Days 6–7: Establish a Consistent Routine

- Set regular times for lights on, lights out, feeding, and interaction.
- Do not rush to bathe the bird, trim its wings, or teach it to talk during this first week.
- If it begins approaching you and taking food from your hand, it is adjusting well.
- Begin recording its weight each day to establish a health baseline.

## Common Adjustment Problems

- **Sudden panicked flight at night (night fright):** Leave a dim night-light on and do not seal the cage completely with the cover.
- **Refusing food:** Check whether its diet changed; mix in the previous food for a gradual transition.
- **Repeated wing trembling:** The bird may be anxious or cold. Check the room temperature and make sure there is no direct draft.

> Tip: If the parrot refuses food, keeps its eyes closed and sleeps excessively, remains fluffed up, or breathes rapidly, contact an avian or exotic-pet veterinarian as soon as possible. Do not wait.
