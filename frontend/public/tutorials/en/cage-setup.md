# Cage and Toy Setup

The cage is your parrot’s “home,” where it spends much of every day. A well-planned setup reduces stress, helps prevent obesity, and avoids injury. This guide explains cage size and how to arrange perches and toys.

## Cage Size

- Bigger is better, with width taking priority over height because parrots fly horizontally.
- Budgerigars and lovebirds: at least 60 × 40 cm.
- Cockatiels and conures: at least 80 × 50 cm.
- Medium and large parrots: begin with at least 100 × 60 cm.
- Bar spacing must be narrower than the bird’s head to prevent entrapment; for small birds, use spacing of 1–1.5 cm.

## Perches

Both the number and material of the perches matter:

- Provide 3–5 perches per cage at staggered heights, positioned so they do not block access to food or water.
- Use varied materials, such as natural branches (apple or willow), nail-conditioning stone, and rope perches.
- Choose diameters that allow the feet to wrap around roughly two-thirds of each perch; a perch that is too thin or too thick can injure the feet.
- Avoid using only smooth plastic perches, which can contribute to pressure sores and bumblefoot.
- Do not position a perch directly over a food or water dish, where droppings could contaminate it.

## Choosing Toys

Parrots need three types of toys:

1. **Destructible toys:** Paper, rattan balls, willow rings, and pine cones satisfy the urge to chew.
2. **Foraging toys:** Puzzle boxes that conceal food provide mental exercise.
3. **Interactive or sound-making toys:** Bells and strings of beads can be used, but check whether small components could be swallowed.

- Rotate some of the toys every week to keep the environment interesting.
- Do not fill the cage all at once; leave enough room for movement.
- Inspect toys for sharp edges and small parts that could come loose.

## Food and Water Placement

- Keep the food and water dishes separate so that food does not fall into the water.
- Place them in the lower-middle area on one side of the cage for easy access.
- Do not put them directly beneath a perch.
- Ceramic or stainless-steel dishes are best because they resist chewing and are easy to clean.

## Where to Place the Cage

- Choose a location with natural light but no direct sun.
- Place one side against a wall to create a sense of security rather than leaving all four sides exposed.
- Keep the cage roughly at human eye level; a cage that is too low can make the bird nervous.
- Keep it away from the kitchen, balcony doors, and air-conditioner vents.
- A cage cover can be used at night to create a feeling of safety.

## Safety Checklist

Check the following once a week:

- Is the cage-door latch loose? Parrots can become very skilled at opening cage doors.
- Are any welds cracked or rusty?
- Have toy ropes become wrapped or knotted in a way that could entangle a foot?
- Are any perches loose enough to fall and strike the bird?
- Is the bottom grate bent, or could a claw become trapped in it?

## Housing Multiple Birds

- Never place unfamiliar birds directly into the same cage; let them observe one another from separate cages first.
- The cage must be large enough for every bird to have “its own corner.”
- Provide several food and water dishes to reduce competition.
- Separate the birds immediately if you see signs of fighting.

> Tip: Parrots get bored, too. A cage placed where the bird can regularly see you pass by and watch family activity meets its social needs better than an isolated corner.
