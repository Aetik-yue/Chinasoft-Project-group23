# Troubleshooting Feather Plucking

A parrot pulling out its own feathers, known as feather plucking, is a common and difficult problem that often has more than one underlying cause. This guide provides a step-by-step approach to identifying the cause instead of trying medications at random.

## First Decide: Medical or Behavioral?

- **Medical:** Feathers are chewed or broken, the skin is inflamed, or parasites or fungi are present. Flaking, redness, and swelling commonly occur as well.
- **Behavioral:** Feathers are pulled out intact while the skin itself appears normal. This is often associated with boredom, anxiety, or breeding-season hormones.

Both types can occur at the same time, so each possible cause needs to be ruled out.

## Step 1: Rule Out Health Problems

Take the bird to an avian or exotic-pet clinic for a basic examination:

- Fecal testing to check for parasites and intestinal infection.
- Skin scraping to check for mites and fungi.
- Complete blood count to check for inflammation and liver or kidney problems.
- Testing for psittacosis (chlamydiosis), which can cause abnormal feathers.

If an illness is responsible, feather plucking will often improve after the underlying disease is treated.

## Step 2: Investigate Environmental Factors

Environmental stress is one of the most common causes of feather plucking:

- **Cage too small:** The bird cannot fly or exercise, and frustration leads to self-injury. Provide a larger cage.
- **Boredom:** There are too few toys or no destructible toys. Offer paper, willow, pine cones, and other safe items to chew.
- **Disrupted light cycle:** Inconsistent daily periods of light and darkness disrupt hormones. Provide 10–12 hours of dark, uninterrupted sleep.
- **Dry air:** Dry, itchy feathers can trigger plucking. Offer more fine misting or increase humidity.
- **Noise or fright:** The bird remains in a stressful environment for long periods.

## Step 3: Investigate Nutritional Problems

- A long-term single-seed diet can cause deficiencies in vitamin A and essential fatty acids.
- The calcium-to-phosphorus ratio may be unbalanced.
- Change to a diet based on pellets, with fresh vegetables and fruit plus a small amount of seed, and observe for 4–6 weeks.

## Step 4: Behavioral Intervention

After health and environmental problems have been ruled out:

- Increase daily interaction and use training to provide mental exercise.
- Offer foraging toys and hide food so that the bird has to “work” for it.
- Reduce long periods of idle waiting by playing soft background sound during the day.
- Do not react loudly when the bird plucks, which could reinforce the behavior.
- In severe cases, consult an avian behavior specialist or veterinarian and consider temporary protection with an Elizabethan collar.

## Common Misconceptions

- Feather plucking is not simply a “bad habit” that punishment will fix; punishment makes anxiety worse.
- Do not apply human skin medication at random, as it may be toxic.
- Do not expect the problem to resolve within a few days. Behavioral feather plucking often takes months to improve.
- Feathers may not regrow in established bald areas if the follicles have been damaged.

## When Veterinary Care Is Essential

- The bird plucks until it bleeds or the skin ulcerates.
- Plucking is accompanied by weight loss or lethargy.
- The condition worsens sharply over a short period.
- The bird begins self-mutilating, such as biting its toes or flesh.

> Tip: Early intervention works best. Feather plucking becomes harder to correct the longer it continues, so act at the first signs instead of waiting for a large bald patch.
