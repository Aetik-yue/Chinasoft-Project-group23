# Nighttime Lighting and Sleep Management

Parrots need enough high-quality sleep. Poor sleep can disrupt hormones, increase irritability, weaken immunity, and even contribute to feather plucking. This guide explains how to manage lighting and the sleep environment.

## How Much Sleep Does a Parrot Need?

- Most parrots need 10–12 hours of sleep each day.
- Young birds and molting birds may need more, around 12–14 hours.
- Brief daytime naps are normal.
- Consistently getting fewer than 10 hours can lead to behavior problems.

## Why a Consistent Light Cycle Matters

Parrots use light to distinguish day from night, and a regular light cycle directly regulates their hormones:

- Open the curtains in the morning so the bird can sense sunrise.
- Turn the lights off at a consistent time in the evening to create darkness.
- Do not switch lights on repeatedly during the night, as this interrupts the sleep cycle.
- If your home remains bright at night, use a cage cover for darkness and move the cage to a dark room.

## How to Use a Cage Cover

- A cage cover blocks light and drafts and creates a sense of security.
- Cover three sides and leave one side open for ventilation; never seal the cage completely.
- A thin cover is enough in summer; a thicker one can be used in winter.
- Cover the cage at a consistent time, such as 9:00 p.m. every night.
- Wash the cover regularly because feather dust accumulates on it.

## Should You Leave a Night-Light On?

- A very dim night-light is recommended for parrots prone to “night frights,” when they suddenly panic and fly in the dark.
- Place the night-light away from the cage and keep it as dim as possible.
- A healthy bird without night frights does not need one; full darkness supports deeper sleep.
- Do not use a bright blue or white night-light.

## Environmental Noise

- Parrots have sensitive hearing, and noise can wake them during sleep.
- Place the cage in a room that stays quiet at night whenever possible.
- If household members are active at night, use sound-dampening curtains or move the bird to another room.
- Keep television and gaming volume low; sound can travel through walls.

## Light-Source Safety

- Do not position the cage directly in front of an intense light source, spotlight, or direct sunlight.
- Avoid flickering lights; parrots can perceive flicker that people may not notice.
- A full-spectrum lamp made for birds may be used during the day to simulate daylight, but it must be switched off at night.
- Replace aging fluorescent lamps promptly because they may begin to flicker.

## Establishing a Sleep Routine

- Turn lights on and off at fixed times, including on weekends whenever possible.
- Reduce activity for 30 minutes before lights-out to create a bedtime routine.
- Stop interacting after lights-out and allow the bird to settle quietly.
- Do not wake it suddenly with loud noise in the morning; let it wake naturally.

## Consequences of Sleep Deprivation

- Hormonal disruption, which may trigger abnormal reproductive behavior such as egg laying or mounting objects.
- Irritability and increased aggression.
- Reduced immune function and greater susceptibility to illness.
- A stronger tendency toward feather plucking.

> Tip: If you stay active late at night, prepare a separate “sleep cage” in the darkest, quietest room. Move the bird there at night and return it to the daytime activity cage in the morning.
