# Daily Feeding and Meal Planning

Parrots cannot live on seeds alone. A limited long-term diet can cause obesity, fatty liver disease, and vitamin deficiencies. This guide explains how to assemble a reasonably balanced daily menu.

## Recommended Food Proportions

A healthy diet can be divided approximately as follows:

- **Pellets:** 60–70% of the diet as the nutritionally balanced staple food.
- **Fresh vegetables and fruit:** 20–30% to provide vitamins and moisture.
- **Seeds and nuts:** 5–10%, offered as treats or training rewards.
- **Protein supplements:** 1–2 times per week, such as cooked egg yolk, boiled chicken breast, or unsalted tofu.

## Choosing Pellets

- Choose a brand with no artificial colors or added sugar.
- Match pellet size to the parrot’s size: medium pellets for conures and cockatiels, and small pellets for budgerigars.
- Change foods gradually: begin with 75% of the old food and 25% of the new food, transitioning over 1–2 weeks.
- Do not give up simply because the bird “does not like it.” Most parrots eventually accept pellets.

## Suitable Vegetables and Fruit

- **Fruit:** Apple with the seeds removed, blueberries, mango, papaya, and kiwi in small amounts because of its high sugar content.
- **Vegetables:** Carrot, pumpkin, broccoli, bell pepper, corn, peas, and leafy greens.
- Fruit is high in sugar, so the total daily amount should not exceed 5–10% of the bird’s body weight.
- Vegetables, especially dark leafy greens, may be offered more generously.

## Foods to Avoid

See *Common Toxic Foods for Parrots*. In particular, remember:

- Avocado, chocolate, coffee, and alcohol.
- Onion, garlic, and raw beans.
- Human foods high in salt, sugar, or fat.
- Fruit pits and seeds, including apple seeds and cherry pits.

## Feeding Schedule

- Offer two main meals each day: replace the food in the morning and add another portion in the early evening.
- Discard uneaten moist food, such as fruit and vegetables, within two hours to prevent spoilage.
- Wash the food dish every day to prevent bacterial growth.
- Use small amounts of high-value treats, such as hemp or sunflower seeds, during training, but subtract them from the bird’s total daily allowance.

## Weight Management

- Weigh the bird regularly; a steady increase in weight is a warning sign for obesity.
- Signs of obesity include a thickened fat pad over the chest, difficulty flying, and breathlessness.
- Weight loss must be gradual. Never withhold food suddenly; slowly reduce seeds and nuts while increasing vegetables, fruit, and activity.
- Being underweight is equally dangerous. A clearly prominent keel bone or sunken chest requires investigation for illness.

## Needs at Different Life Stages

- **Young birds:** Need a high-protein, high-fat weaning diet offered in small, frequent meals.
- **Molting birds:** Need more protein and calcium; see *Molting Care Essentials*.
- **Breeding birds:** Need more calcium and protein, but this high level of nutrition should not continue outside the breeding period.
- **Senior birds:** Need less fat and more easily digested soft foods.

> Tip: Schedule one “light-food day” each week with only pellets and vegetables to support digestive health and weight control.
