# Common Mistakes for New Parrot Owners

New parrot owners can easily fall into many traps. This guide collects the most common misconceptions so that you can avoid detours, unnecessary expense, and suffering for your bird.

## Mistake 1: Feeding Only Sunflower Seeds and Other Seeds

- A long-term diet of only sunflower seeds or millet can cause obesity, fatty liver disease, and vitamin A deficiency.
- Seeds should be treats only; the staple diet should consist of pellets plus vegetables and fruit.
- See *Daily Feeding and Meal Planning*.

## Mistake 2: A Smaller Cage Is Easier to Clean and Therefore Better

- A cage that is too small can cause stress, obesity, and feather plucking.
- Width matters more than height because parrots fly horizontally.
- See *Cage and Toy Setup* when choosing the size.

## Mistake 3: Carrying the Bird Everywhere on Your Shoulder

- The shoulder becomes the bird’s “safe high ground,” which can encourage possessive behavior.
- Carrying a bird on your shoulder outdoors creates a very high risk of escape.
- It is better to teach the bird to step onto a finger or a freestanding perch.

## Mistake 4: Using Newspaper or Sand as Cage Liner

- Long-term contact with newspaper ink can present a risk.
- Sand may be swallowed accidentally, and its dust can injure the respiratory tract.
- Kitchen paper or purpose-made cage paper is safer.

## Mistake 5: Assuming “If It Does Not Eat, It Cannot Be a Picky Eater”

- Parrots are sensitive to food color and shape and may select only one type.
- A bird may appear to eat a lot while still receiving very limited nutrition.
- Observe what it actually consumes, not simply how much food disappears from the dish.

## Mistake 6: Treating Illness with Human Medication

- The dosage or ingredients in human medicines may be toxic to birds.
- Misusing “anti-inflammatory medicine” can mask the illness and promote drug resistance.
- An avian or exotic-pet veterinarian is the appropriate source of treatment.

## Mistake 7: Assuming One Wing Trim Solves the Problem Permanently

- New flight feathers grow after a trim, allowing the bird to fly again.
- Wing trimming does not mean windows can be left open; a startled bird may still fly a long distance.
- Environmental management plus training is more reliable than wing trimming alone.

## Mistake 8: The More Sun, the Better

- Moderate exposure to gentle morning sunlight is beneficial.
- Direct midday sun can cause heatstroke.
- Always provide shade so the bird can move out of the sun by itself.

## Mistake 9: Using Fragrance Diffusers, Essential Oils, or Mosquito Coils

- Parrots have extremely sensitive respiratory systems, and many “natural” essential oils are toxic to birds.
- Mosquito coils and insecticides are even more dangerous; move the bird away before using them.
- Remove indoor odors through ventilation instead of masking them with fragrance.

## Mistake 10: Never Trimming Nails and Waiting for “Natural Wear”

- The home environment often provides too little natural wear, allowing nails to become overgrown.
- Long nails can catch on fabric and cause fractures.
- Inspect and trim them regularly.

## Mistake 11: Changing Food Too Often

- Parrots have sensitive digestive systems, and frequent diet changes can cause diarrhea.
- Transition to a new food over 1–2 weeks.
- Once you have chosen a suitable staple food, use it consistently.

## Mistake 12: Casually Placing the Bird in a Cage with Another Bird

- Unfamiliar birds placed directly together may fight and become injured.
- Let them observe each other in separate cages for several weeks first.
- A shared cage must provide adequate space and enough food and water stations.

## Mistake 13: Ignoring Weight Records

- Parrots hide illness, and weight loss may be an early warning sign.
- Without weighing, the problem is often discovered too late.
- Weigh the bird at least twice a week.

## Mistake 14: Using Hunger as a Training Method

- Training through food deprivation damages health and trust.
- A small amount of treat shortly before the normal meal is sufficient.
- Make sure the bird eats enough after training.

## Mistake 15: Assuming “Eating and Passing Droppings Means It Is Healthy”

- Eating and passing droppings are basic functions, not proof of complete health.
- You must also observe alertness, feathers, breathing, and weight trends.
- A reliable assessment considers all of these factors together.

> Tip: Keeping a bird is a long-term learning process. Do not rely on scattered “clever tricks” from the internet. When in doubt, consult authoritative sources or an avian or exotic-pet veterinarian instead of experimenting on your own bird.
