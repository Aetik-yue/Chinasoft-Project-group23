# Keeping a Parrot Warm Before and After a Medicated Bath

Medicated baths are sometimes used to treat feather lice, excessive dander, or fungal problems. Parrots have a high body temperature and rely on their feathers for insulation, so becoming chilled while wet can easily lead to illness or respiratory disease. Maintaining warmth is central to safe treatment; this guide covers preparation, bathing, and aftercare.

## When a Medicated Bath Is Needed

A healthy parrot normally needs only a fine mist of clean water or a shallow bathing dish. Use a medicated bath only on veterinary advice and in situations such as:

- Frequent feather plucking with obvious flaking.
- Abnormal discharge on the skin or feathers.
- A confirmed external parasite infestation, such as feather lice or mites.
- A veterinarian has prescribed a targeted medicated-bath plan.

Do not give frequent medicated baths simply “to make the feathers shinier.” Overuse can strip the feathers of their natural oils.

## Preparing for the Bath

- Keep the room at 25–28°C and switch off fans or direct air-conditioning drafts.
- Prepare warm water at about 30–35°C. Test it against the inside of your wrist; it should feel warm, never hot.
- Use the medication concentration recommended by the veterinarian and never increase it arbitrarily.
- Choose a shallow basin with water no higher than the lower legs to reduce the risk of aspiration.
- Prepare the hair dryer, towel, and warming lamp in advance so that aftercare can begin immediately.

## During the Medicated Bath

- Let the parrot stand and splash by itself; do not force it down or immerse its head.
- Limit the bath to 5–10 minutes, as a longer bath can cause loss of body heat.
- Stop immediately if the bird strongly resists, shivers, or begins breathing with its beak open.
- Keep medicated water out of the eyes and nostrils. If necessary, use a small spray bottle held low rather than immersing the bird.

## Warmth After the Bath: The Most Important Step

1. Use a soft towel to blot away surface moisture without rubbing hard.
2. Dry the bird from a safe distance with a hair dryer on its low-heat setting, gently parting the feathers by hand so that the inner layers dry completely.
3. Once dry, let the bird rest in a warm, quiet place for 1–2 hours.
4. Keep it away from drafts and air-conditioned rooms for at least two hours after the bath.
5. Do not allow a second bath that day.

## Warning Signs

- Shivering that continues for more than 30 minutes after the bath.
- Sneezing or nasal discharge.
- Lethargy or refusal of food.
- Red skin or new flaking.

> Tip: Avoid medicated baths in winter whenever possible. If one is essential, perform it at the warmest time around midday and raise the room temperature above 28°C.
