# First-Aid Kit and Common Emergencies

When an accident happens, correct early care can buy valuable time before veterinary treatment. This guide explains how to prepare a parrot first-aid kit and the basic response to several common emergencies.

## What to Keep in the First-Aid Kit

- **Styptic powder:** For bleeding caused by nail trimming or injury.
- **Disinfectant:** F10 or diluted povidone-iodine, not alcohol.
- **Gauze and cotton swabs:** For cleaning and bandaging.
- **Small towel:** For gentle restraint and wrapping.
- **Heating pad or disposable hand warmer:** For warmth during hypothermia; always place cloth between it and the bird.
- **Travel carrier:** For emergency transport.
- **Electrolyte powder:** For rehydration after diarrhea or heatstroke.
- **Syringe without a needle:** For giving water or medication.
- **Hemostats and small scissors:** For removing entangling material.
- Contact details for an avian or exotic-pet clinic and a 24-hour emergency number.

## Treating Bleeding

1. Locate the source and determine whether the bleeding comes from a nail, a feather, or another wound.
2. Press gauze firmly against the bleeding point for 2–3 minutes.
3. If pressure alone does not stop it, apply styptic powder and continue pressing.
4. A bleeding feather, caused by damage to a blood feather, may require the entire feather to be removed. Grip the base with hemostats and pull it out smoothly in the direction of growth.
5. Seek emergency veterinary care if bleeding is heavy or will not stop.

## Collision or Fall Injuries

- Gently move the bird into a quiet, warm cage or travel carrier.
- Do not force an examination, which could cause further injury.
- Observe whether it can stand and whether its head tilts or one wing droops.
- Veterinary care is recommended even if no injury is visible, because internal injuries may not be apparent at first.
- Restrict activity for 24 hours and prevent vigorous flight.

## Entanglement and Strangulation

- If a toy rope is wrapped around the foot or neck, carefully cut the rope with scissors first.
- Do not pull hard, as this can tighten the loop.
- After freeing the bird, check for pressure marks and bruising.
- Severe constriction injuries require veterinary assessment of circulation.

## Poisoning

See *Common Toxic Foods for Parrots*:

1. Remove the toxic substance immediately and record what was consumed, how much, and when.
2. Do not induce vomiting yourself.
3. Bring the remaining substance or packaging and seek veterinary care immediately.
4. Keep the bird warm and quiet during transport.

## Heatstroke

See *Summer Cooling and Winter Warmth*:

1. Move the bird to a shaded, cool, well-ventilated place.
2. Apply a fine mist to the feet and undersides of the wings.
3. Do not pour ice water over the bird.
4. Arrange a veterinary examination after it stabilizes.

## Hypothermia

- Fluffed feathers, shivering, and lethargy may indicate hypothermia.
- Move the bird to a warm room at 25–28°C.
- Wrap it in a towel or provide gentle heat from a heating pad through a layer of cloth.
- Do not aim a hot hair dryer directly at it.
- Veterinary care is still recommended after the bird warms up so that the cause can be investigated.

## Oil on the Feathers

- Cooking oil or machine oil on a parrot interferes with insulation and preening.
- Do not use solvents for cleaning.
- Use cornstarch or unscented talcum powder to absorb the oil, working it gently through the feathers several times.
- Heavy oil contamination must be treated by a veterinarian; washing it yourself may damage the skin.

## Preparing for the Veterinary Visit

- Transport the bird in a travel carrier lined with a towel.
- Bring a recent droppings sample if diarrhea is present.
- Record when the symptoms began and how they have changed.
- Bring a list of all medicines and supplements currently being given.
- Call ahead to confirm that the clinic is open.

## Everyday Prevention

- Inspect the cage and toys regularly for safety hazards.
- Keep doors and windows closed to prevent collisions and escape.
- Move the parrot away from the kitchen while cooking.
- Put away small parts, strings, and toxic plants.

> Tip: Store the emergency contact list with the first-aid kit, make sure everyone in the household knows where it is, and check medication expiration dates every six months.
